Reciprocal Identities

Pythagorean Identities
\[ \sin^2{\theta} + \cos^2{\theta} = 1 \] \[ \tan^2{\theta} + 1 = \sec^2{\theta} \] \[ \cot^2{\theta} + 1 = \csc^2{\theta} \]
Double Angle Formulas
\[ \sin{(2\theta)} = 2\sin{\theta}\cos{\theta} \] \[ \begin{align} \cos{(2\theta)} &= \cos^2{\theta} - \sin^2{\theta} \\ &= 2\cos^2{\theta} - 1 \\ &= 1 - 2\sin^2{\theta} \end{align} \] \[ \tan{(2\theta)} = \dfrac{2\tan{\theta}}{1 - \tan^2{\theta}} \]
Half Angle Formulas
\[ \sin{\theta} = \pm \sqrt{\dfrac{1 - \cos{(2\theta)}}{2}} \] \[ \cos{\theta} = \pm \sqrt{\dfrac{1 + \cos{(2\theta)}}{2}} \] \[ \tan{\theta} = \pm \sqrt{\dfrac{1 - \cos{(2\theta)}}{1 + \cos{(2\theta)}}} \]
Sum/Difference Formulas
\[ \sin{(\alpha \pm \beta)} = \sin{\alpha} \cos{\beta} \pm \cos{\alpha} \sin{\beta} \] \[ \cos{(\alpha \pm \beta)} = \cos{\alpha} \cos{\beta} \mp \sin{\alpha} \sin{\beta} \] \[ \tan{(\alpha \pm \beta)} = \dfrac{\tan{\alpha} \pm \tan{\beta}}{1 \mp \tan{\alpha} \tan{\beta}} \]
Product to Sum Formulas
\[ \sin{\alpha}\sin{\beta} = \dfrac{1}{2} \left[ \cos{(\alpha - \beta) - \cos{(\alpha + \beta)}} \right] \] \[ \cos{\alpha}\cos{\beta} = \dfrac{1}{2} \left[ \cos{(\alpha - \beta) + \cos{(\alpha + \beta)}} \right] \] \[ \sin{\alpha}\cos{\beta} = \dfrac{1}{2} \left[ \sin{(\alpha + \beta) + \sin{(\alpha - \beta)}} \right] \] \[ \cos{\alpha}\sin{\beta} = \dfrac{1}{2} \left[ \sin{(\alpha + \beta) - \sin{(\alpha - \beta)}} \right] \]
Sum to Product Formulas
\[ \sin{\alpha} + \sin{\beta} = 2 \sin{\left(\dfrac{\alpha + \beta}{2}\right)} \cos{\left(\dfrac{\alpha - \beta}{2}\right)} \] \[ \sin{\alpha} - \sin{\beta} = 2 \cos{\left(\dfrac{\alpha + \beta}{2}\right)} \sin{\left(\dfrac{\alpha - \beta}{2}\right)} \] \[ \cos{\alpha} + \cos{\beta} = 2 \cos{\left(\dfrac{\alpha + \beta}{2}\right)} \cos{\left(\dfrac{\alpha - \beta}{2}\right)} \] \[ \cos{\alpha} - \cos{\beta} = -2 \sin{\left(\dfrac{\alpha + \beta}{2}\right)} \sin{\left(\dfrac{\alpha - \beta}{2}\right)} \]

Mason's Gain Formula
\[ G = \dfrac{y_{in}}{y_{out}} = \dfrac{ \sum_{k=1}^N G_k \Delta_k }{\Delta} \] \[ \Delta = 1 - \sum L_i + \sum L_i L_j - \sum L_i L_j L_k + \dots \]

AM ▼

General Time-Domain Form
\[ \begin{align} x_{AM}(t) &= A_c \cos{(2 \pi f_c t)} + s(t) \cos{(2 \pi f_c t)} \\ &= \left[ A_c + s(t) \right] \cos{(2 \pi f_c t)} \\ &= A_c \left[ 1 + m_a s_n(t) \right] \cos{(2 \pi f_c t)} \end{align} \] \[ s_n(t) = \dfrac{s(t)}{|\displaystyle \min_t s(t)|}, \quad |\min_t s(t) | \neq 0 \] \[ m_a = \dfrac{|\displaystyle \min_t s(t)|}{A_c}, \quad m_a \leq 1 \]
Note: the normalised signal \(s_n(t) \geq -1\). This implies that \(A_{\min} = A_c(1-m_a) \) and that \( A_{\max} = A_c \max_t \left[ 1 + m_a s_n(t) \right] \). If \(s_n(t)\) is symmetrical in that \( |\max_t s_n(t)| = |\min_t s_n(t)| = 1 \) then \(A_{\max} = A_c(1+m_a) \). This implies that the modulation index \(m_a\) can be computed as \[ m_a = \dfrac{A_{\max} - A_{\min}}{A_{\max} + A_{\min}} \] Further, if \(m_a > 1\) then the envelope in front of \(\cos{(2 \pi f_c t)}\) becomes negative, resulting in signal inversion. This is called overmodulation and is undesirable with conventional AM since a coherent detector is required to recover the modulating message. The condition of \(m_a > 1\) is equivalent to the condition of \( \min_t s(t) < -A_c \). If \(m_a = 1\) and \(s_n(t)\) is symmetrical, then \(x_{AM}(t)\) varies between \(A_{\min} = 0\) and \(A_{\max} = 2A_c\).

Electrostatics ▼

Coulomb's Law
\[ \mathbf{F}_{12} = \dfrac{q_1 q_2 (\mathbf{r}_2 - \mathbf{r}_1)}{4 \pi \varepsilon \left| \mathbf{r}_2 - \mathbf{r}_1 \right|^3} = \dfrac{q_1 q_2 \mathbf{\hat{r}}_{12}}{4 \pi \varepsilon \left| \mathbf{r}_{12} \right|^2} \]
The electrostatic force exerted by a charge \( q_1 \) located at \( \mathbf{r_1} \) on another charge \( q_2 \) located at \( \mathbf{r_2} \). The line of force is specified by the unit vector \( \mathbf{\hat{r}}_{12} \), and the direction depends on the charges (like charges repel, opposite charges attract). This force follows an inverse-square law, where \( F \propto 1 / r^2 \). The force is also modulated by the medium's electrical permittivity \( \varepsilon = \varepsilon_0 \varepsilon_r \).
Force on Charged Particle
\[ \mathbf{F}_C = q \mathbf{E}_C \]
The electrostatic or "Coulomb" force exerted on a particle with charge \( q \) at a particular point due to the electrostatic or "Coulomb" field \( \mathbf{E}_C \) at that point. The Coulomb field is defined as the electrostatic force per unit charge (\( \text{N/C} \)). This can also be expressed as Volts per meter (\( \text{V/m} \)) since \( \text{V=J/C} \) and \( \text{N=J/m} \).
Electric Field
(Single Point Charge at \(O\))
\[ \mathbf{E}_C(\mathbf{r}) = \displaystyle{\lim_{q_t \to 0}} \dfrac{\mathbf{F}_{q_t}}{q_t} = \dfrac{q \mathbf{r}}{4 \pi \varepsilon \left| \mathbf{r} \right|^3} = \dfrac{q \mathbf{\hat{r}}}{4 \pi \varepsilon \left| \mathbf{r} \right|^2} \]
As explained in the equation for "Force on Charged Particle", the Coulomb field is defined as the electrostatic force per unit charge (\( \text{N/C} \)). This is experimentally conceptualised as introducing a small test charge \( q_t \) into an existing electric field, measuring the force it experiences, and then taking the ratio of the force and charge. Technically, the test charge will alter the net electrostatic field with its own field by superposition. Thus, the Coulomb field is theoretically defined as this ratio when \( q_t \) approaches zero, as the ratio then acts as a "test-probe" that doesn't affect or distort the field it's measuring.

By comparing the "Force on Charged Particle" and "Coulomb's Law" formulas, it is clear where the definition of the electric field comes from. In this case, the charge creating the electric field is located at the origin, and the equation provides the electric field at an arbitrary point \( \mathbf{r} \). The expression \( \mathbf{r}_{12} = \mathbf{r}_{2} - \mathbf{r}_{1} \) simplifies to \( \mathbf{r}_{12} = \mathbf{r}_{2} = \mathbf{r} \) since \( \mathbf{r}_{1} = \mathbf{0} \).
Electric Field
(Single Point Charge at \(\mathbf{r'}\))
\[ \mathbf{E}_C(\mathbf{r}) = \dfrac{q (\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r'})}{4 \pi \varepsilon \left| \mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r'} \right|^3} \]
This expression is almost equivalent to "Electric Field (Single Point Charge at \(O\))" but the charge creating the field is now at \(\mathbf{r'}\) instead of the origin. Now, the Coulomb field's intensity drops off with the square of the distance to the source charge, rather than with the square of the distance to the origin.
Electric Field
(Multiple Point Charges)
\[ \mathbf{E}_C(\mathbf{r}) = \dfrac{1}{4 \pi \varepsilon} \displaystyle{\sum_{n=1}^{N}} \dfrac{q_n (\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}_n)}{\left| \mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}_n \right|^3} \]
This equation is an extension of the "Electric Field (Single Point Charge at \(\mathbf{r'}\))" formula, accounting for the contributions of more than one charge to the total Coulomb field. The forces that each charge exert are linear with respect to the charge's magnitude and they are independent of other charges. This means that their effects follow the principle of superposition and can be combined additively.
Electric Field
(Volume Charge Density)
\[ \mathbf{E}_C(\mathbf{r}) = \dfrac{1}{4 \pi \varepsilon} \displaystyle{\int_{V'}} \dfrac{\rho_V(\mathbf{r'}) (\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r'})}{\left| \mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r'} \right|^3} dV' \]
This equation represents a smoothing out of the "Electric Field (Multiple Point Charges)" formula from discrete point charges to continous volume charge densities (Coulombs per cubic metre). A volume charge density could be used to describe a dielectric material in a varying electric field or a cloud of hydrogen ions.

Technically, all charges are discrete point charges, but at a macroscopic scale they can be represented as continuous charge densities. There may be more charge in certain regions, which would correspond to a locally higher charge density. This is why \( \rho_V \) is a function of \( \mathbf{r'} \).

The reason for the dashed notation is that this formula represents the electric field a charged particle would experience at some point \( \mathbf{r} \), which is the integral of the electric field contribution from each infinitesimal charge given by \( dq' = \rho(\mathbf{r'})dV' \). The effect of each infinitesimal charge \( dq' \) at \( \mathbf{r} \) will be inversely proportional to \( (\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r'})^2 \) and will have a line of force along this direction. Thus, the total contribution of the volume charge must be determined with a vector integral over the whole charge volume density.

Note that the formula is not valid for points within the charge volume density.
Electric Field
(Surface Charge Density)
\[ \mathbf{E}_C(\mathbf{r}) = \dfrac{1}{4 \pi \varepsilon} \displaystyle{\int_{S'}} \dfrac{\rho_S(\mathbf{r'}) (\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r'})}{\left| \mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r'} \right|^3} dS' \]
This equation is similar in concept to the "Electric Field (Volume Charge Density)" formula, but for continuous surface charge densities (Coulombs per square metre). A surface charge density could be used to describe a capacitor's plate or a metallic shielding.

Note that the formula is not valid for points on the surface charge density.
Electric Field
(Line Charge Density)
\[ \mathbf{E}_C(\mathbf{r}) = \dfrac{1}{4 \pi \varepsilon} \displaystyle{\int_{L'}} \dfrac{\rho_L(\mathbf{r'}) (\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r'})}{\left| \mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r'} \right|^3} dL' \]
This equation is similar in concept to the "Electric Field (Volume Charge Density)" formula, but for continuous line charge densities (Coulombs per metre). A line charge density could be used to describe the charge on a long thin wire or a beam of charged particles.

Note that the formula is not valid for points on the line charge density.
Work Done on Charged Particle
\[ W = - \displaystyle{\int_{L}} q \mathbf{E}_C \cdot d\mathbf{l} \]
The work (or amount of energy) required to move a charge \( q \) along the path \( L \) in the presence of the Coulomb field \( \mathbf{E}_C \). The \( q \mathbf{E}_C \) part of the expression is the electrostatic force on the charged particle, and work is the product of force and displacement. The dot product captures the degree of alignment between the force and the movement. Movement that is perpendicular to the elctric field does no work, since the charge is moving along a line of equipotential.

The expression being negative should make sense. Imagine if a positively charged particle moves in the same direction as the electric field (so \( \mathbf{l} \) and \( \mathbf{E}_C \) are in the same direction). Then, the expression \( q \mathbf{E}_C \cdot d\mathbf{l} \) would be positive. However, this is how the particle would move naturally, converting its electric potential energy into kinetic energy. Such movement is equivalent to negative work being done on the particle, and the negative sign in front of the integral ensures the work computed with the formula agrees with this logic.
Electric Potential Field
\[ \varphi(\mathbf{r}) = - \displaystyle{\int_{\infty}^{\mathbf{r}}} \mathbf{E}_C \cdot d\mathbf{l} \]
The electric potential field \( \varphi(\mathbf{r}) \) describes the amount of electrostatic potential energy per unit charge a particle has due to being at a particular point \( \mathbf{r} \) in a Coulomb field \( \mathbf{E}_C \). This can be conceptualised as the energy required to move a charge \( q = 1 \ \text{C} \) from an infinite distance (where the source of the electric field exerts no force on the charge) to the point \( \mathbf{r} \).
Electric Field
(Gradient of Potential Field)
\[ \mathbf{E}_C(\mathbf{r}) = - \nabla \varphi(\mathbf{r}) \implies \nabla \times \mathbf{E}_C = \mathbf{0} \]
The Coulomb field \( \mathbf{E}_C \) at a point \( \mathbf{r} \) is equal to the negative gradient of the electric potential field \( \varphi \) at that point \( \mathbf{r} \).

Imagine bringing a positive test charge from an infinite distance towards a positive source charge. As the test charge approaches the source, its potential energy increases. Its potential energy will increase fastest if it is moved radially towards the source of the field, so this direction defines the gradient of the electric potential field. Furthermore, this gradient will be steeper near the source, since the inverse-square nature of the electrostatic force implies that forcing an equivalent displacement closer to the source will require more work. Therefore, it should make sense that the electric field is in the opposite direction to the gradient of the potential field, and also that the magnitude of the electric field is equal to the magnitude of this gradient.

As the Coulomb field can be defined as a gradient field, this mathematically implies that the curl of the Coulomb field is zero (see the "Curl of a Gradient Field" formula). By definition, the electric field points radially from higher to lower force at each point in space and has no rotational component. Another way to think about this is that a point charge has a radial field, and all complex electrostatic fields can be decomposed into a superposition (or sum) of radial fields caused by point charges. Thus, an electrostatic field has no rotational components and must have a curl of zero at all points.
Electric Potential Difference
\[ V_{\mathbf{ab}} = \dfrac{W_{\mathbf{a} \to \mathbf{b}}}{q} = \varphi(\mathbf{b}) - \varphi(\mathbf{a}) = - \displaystyle{\int_{\mathbf{a}}^{\mathbf{b}}} \mathbf{E}_C \cdot d\mathbf{l} \]
The electric potential difference \( V \) between points \( \mathbf{a} \) and \( \mathbf{b} \) is the amount of work per unit charge (Joules per Coulomb) required to move a charge from point \( \mathbf{a} \) to point \( \mathbf{b} \) in the presence of a Coulomb field \( \mathbf{E}_C \). The integral form of the formula comes from the "Work Done on Charged Particle" formula, where \( L \) is any path from \( \mathbf{a} \) to \( \mathbf{b} \), and represents integrating the dot product of electrostatic force and displacement over the line path that was taken. The electric potential form of the formula comes from the idea that the work required to move a unit of charge from \( \mathbf{a} \) to \( \mathbf{b} \) is equivalent to moving a unit of charge from \( \mathbf{a} \) out to some infinite distance and then back in to \( \mathbf{b} \).
Conservative Coulomb Field
\[ \displaystyle{\oint} \mathbf{E}_C \cdot d\mathbf{l} = 0 \]
The Coulomb field is conservative, which means that the amount of work required to move a unit of charge between two points is path-invariant. This property is summarised by the fact that the integral of the dot product of electrostatic force and displacement around any closed path in a Coulomb field is always zero. If this integral was ever negative, then energy would disappear from the universe. If it was ever positive, then infinite energy could be created from nothing. Therefore, this property must be true, or the First Law of Thermodynamics would be violated.
Gauss's Law
(Integral Form)
(Free Space)
\[ \Psi = \displaystyle{\oint_S} \mathbf{E}_C \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \dfrac{1}{\varepsilon_0} \oint_V \rho_V dV = \dfrac{q}{\varepsilon_0} \]
Gauss's Law states that the amount of electric flux \( \Psi \) emerging through a closed surface \( S \) is directly proportional to the net charge \( q \) contained within \( S \), and the proportionality constant in free space is \( 1/\varepsilon_0 \) (where \( \varepsilon_0 \) is the electric permittivity of free space). The amount of flux emerging through the surface can be computed by integrating \( \mathbf{E}_C \cdot d\mathbf{S} \) over the whole surface \( S \), where \( d\mathbf{S} \) represents the normal vector to some infinitesimally small region of \( S \). This dot product captures the compoent of the electric field perpendicularly exiting the infinitesimal region of the surface.

It is imporant to note that the emerging electric flux is proportional to the net enclosed charge, regardless of the charge distribution. This means that there may be regions of both positive and negative charges enclosed by the surface, and they may be located anywhere within the enclosed volume. Thus, the ratio \( q / \varepsilon_0 \) can be equivalently represented by the integral of the enclosed volume's charge density \( \rho_V \) over the entire enlcosed volume \( V \), scaled by the permittivity of free space. See the "Electric Field (Volume Charge Density)" formula for more on volume charge densities.
Gauss's Law
(Differential Form)
(Free Space)
\[ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{E}_C = - \nabla^2 \varphi = \displaystyle{\lim_{\Delta V \to 0}} \dfrac{\Psi_V}{\Delta V} = \dfrac{\rho_V}{\varepsilon_0} \]
Gauss's Law in differential or point form states that the divergence of the electric field at a point is directly proportional to the charge density at that point, and the proportionality constant in free space is \( 1/\varepsilon_0 \) (where \( \varepsilon_0 \) is the electric permittivity of free space).

Conceptually, the divergence of the electric field at a point can be thought of as the amount of electric flux emerging from a small volume as that volume shrinks and approaches a point. Physically, this amount of flux would always tend to zero at all points in space unless the point happens to be the precise location of a point charge. In this case, each point charge in space can be considered a singularity of charge density, which can be repesented by the expression \(\rho(\mathbf{r}_i) = q_i \delta(\mathbf{r}_i) \). Although Gauss's Law in point form may seem less useful than the integral form, it highlights the idea that all electric field lines diverge out from or converge into the locations of point charges, reflecting the fact that these points act as sources (positive charges) or sinks (negative charges) of the electric field.

Since the electric field can be described as the negative gradient of the electric potential field (\( \mathbf{E}_C = -\nabla \varphi\)), the divergence of the electric field can also be described as the negative Laplacian of the electric potential field, since \( \nabla \cdot (-\nabla) = -\nabla^2 \). See "Laplacian of a Scalar Field (Divergence of a Gradient Field)" and "Poisson's Equation" for more.
Poisson's Equation
\[ \nabla^2 \varphi = - \dfrac{\rho_V}{\varepsilon_0} \]
Poisson's equation relates the Laplacian of the electric potential field \( \varphi \) to the distribution of charges in space \( \rho \) (charge density). The Laplacian of a scalar field is the sum of the second derivatives of the field. As explained in "Gauss's Law (Differential Form) (Free Space)", the volume charge density is zero at all points in free space except at the locations of point charges. Thus, Poisson's equation simplifies to Laplace's equation in the space between point charges. Both Laplace's equation and Poisson's equation are examples of elliptic partial differential equations, which are a class of second-order PDEs that describe static fields or equilibriums (like an electrostatic or Coulomb field).

Given the charge distribution, solving Poisson's equation gives the electric potential field, which can be used to compute the electric or Coulomb field. Conversely, if the electric potential is fixed by some external source, then the resulting charge distribution can be computed using Poisson's equation.
Electric Dipole Moment
(Actual)
\[\mathbf{p} = q \mathbf{s}\]
The moment of an electric dipole is a vector quantity that captures the polarity of two equal but opposite charges (\( +q \) and \( -q \)) that are separated in space by a displacement of \( \mathbf{s} \). However, most microscopic dipoles are considered as "ideal dipoles" where the physical dimensions of the dipole are ignored and the dipole is treated as a point-like object. See "Electric Dipole Moment (Idealised)" for more.
Electric Dipole Moment
(Idealised)
\[\mathbf{p} = \lim \limits_{\substack{q \to \infty \\ \mathbf{s} \to 0}} q \mathbf{s}\]
A real electric dipole has physical dimensions and its electric dipole moment is described in "Electric Dipole Moment (Actual)". However, it can be simpler to treat microscopic electric dipoles as point-like objects, where the tiny dimensions of the dipole are ignored. This can be conceptualised as taking the actual dipole moment, and then simultaneously decreasing the displacement whilst increasing the charge in such a way that the quantity \( |q \mathbf{s}| \) remains equal to the magnitude of the true dipole moment. As the separation approaches zero, the dipole becomes point-like. This does result in a slightly different electric field, but it is practically equivalent on a macroscopic scale.
Dipole Density Field
(Definition)
\[\mathbf{P} = \displaystyle{\lim_{\Delta V \to 0}} \dfrac{\sum_{i (\Delta V)} \mathbf{p}_i}{\Delta V} \]
The dipole density field is a continuous or smooth macroscopic representation of the electric dipole moments caused by microscopic dipoles within a material. Formally, the value of the dipole density field at a point can be conceptualised as the vector sum of all the dipoles \( \mathbf{p}_i \) within some volume \( \Delta V \) (centred on that point) as the size of that volume tends to zero.
Dipole Density Field
(Linear Isotropic Dielectrics)
\[\mathbf{P} = \varepsilon_0 \chi \mathbf{E} \]
A dielectric is a material with low conductivity due to the charges within the material being locally bound. A linear isotropic dielectric is a material where, when an external electric field is applied to the material, the charges in the material polarise in (approximately) linear proportion to the electric field strength and the degree of polarisation is equal regardless of the applied electric field's direction. In such a material, the dipole density field can be entirely characterised by the compound constant of porportionality \( \varepsilon_0 \chi\), where \( \chi \) is a dimensionless quantity called the susceptibility of the material.

Realistically, no material is perfectly linear and isotropic. Materials are usually only approximately linear over a certain range of electric field intensities, and isotropy generally emerges at a macroscopic scale in materials with small regions of anisotropy where the direction of anisotropy are random. Typically, values of \( \chi \) must be specified for a particular range of electric field frequencies and intensities. Examples of materials that are often modelled as linear isotropic dielectrics include air, glass, water, ice, porcelain, soil and most plastics.

Probability Theory ▼

Basic Probability Concepts ▼

Conditional Probability
\[ P(A|B) = \dfrac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)} \]
The probability of event \(A\) occurring given that event \(B\) has occurred is equal to the probability that both \(A\) and \(B\) would occur divided by the probability that \(B\) would occur. Think about randomly drawing an element from a Venn Diagram for events \(A\) and \(B\): conditioning on \(B\) means that the only values \(A\) can take must exist inside the circle for \(B\), implying they must exist in the intersection between the circles for \(A\) and \(B\). The probability of selecting an element of \(A\) given you're drawing from all elements of \(B\) is then clearly the intersection between \(A\) and \(B\) over all of \(B\).
Independent Events
\[ P(A|B) = P(A) \Rightarrow P(A \cap B) = P(A) P(B) \]
If event \(A\) occurring is entirely independent of the occurrence of event \(B\), then the probability of event \(A\) occuring given that event \(B\) has occurred is simply the probability that \(A\) event occurs. For example, the probabilty that you get a six when you roll a fair die is one sixth, regardless of whether you got a six in the previous roll. This also implies that the probability of \(A\) and \(B\) occurring is equal to the probability of \(A\) occurring multiplied by the probability of \(B\) occurring: \[ P(A|B) = \dfrac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)} = P(A) \Rightarrow P(A \cap B) = P(A) P(B) \]
Law of Total Probability
\[ P(A) = \sum_i P(A|B_i) P(B_i) = \sum_i P(A \cap B_i)\]
This equation holds when the set \(B_n : n = 1, 2, \dots, n\) is a set of mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive events. For example, the probability that you get heads when you flip a coin is equal to the probability of getting heads given your previous flip was heads plus the probability of getting heads given your previous flip was tails.
Bayes' Theorem
\[ P(A|B) = \dfrac{P(B|A) P(A)}{P(B)} \]
Bayes' theorem can be derived from the definition of a conditional probability: \[ P(A|B) = \dfrac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)} \Rightarrow P(A \cap B) = P(A|B) P(A) \] Similarly, \[ P(B|A) = \dfrac{P(A \cap B)}{P(A)} \Rightarrow P(A \cap B) = P(B|A) P(B) \] Equating gives: \[ P(A|B) = \dfrac{P(B|A) P(A)}{P(B)} \] The Bayesian interpretation of Bayes' theorem is often summarised as: \[ posterior = \dfrac{(likelihood)(prior)}{evidence} \] Note that \(P(B)\) must be non-zero.
Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF)
\[ F_X(x) = P(X \leq x) \text{ (single)}\] \[ F_{XY}(x, y) = P(X \leq x, Y \leq y) \text{ (joint)}\] \[ F_\mathbf{X}(\mathbf{x}) = P(X_1 \leq x_1, X_2 \leq x_2, \dots, X_n \leq x_n) \text{ (vector)}\]
The CDF of a real-valued random variable \(X\) evaluated at \(x\) represents the probability that the random variable \(X\) will be less than or equal to \(x\). For continuous random variables, it represents the accumulation of the area under the probability density function (PDF). For discrete random variables, it represents the accumulation of the probabilities of each discrete outcome in the probability mass function (PMF).

Key Properties:
\[\text{Bounds: } F_X( - \infty) = 0, \quad F_X( + \infty) = 1 \] \[\text{Monotonic: } F_X(x_1) \leq F_X(x_2) \text{ when } x_1 < x_2 \] \[\text{Complimentary Probabilities: } P(X > x) = 1 - F_X(x) \] \[\text{Range of Outcomes: } P(x_1 < X \leq x_2) = F_X(x_2) - F_X(x_1) \]
Probability Mass Function (PMF)
\[ p_X(x) = P(X = x) \text{ (single)}\] \[ p_{XY}(x,y) = P(X = x, Y = y) \text{ (joint)}\] \[ p_{\mathbf{X}}(\mathbf{x}) = P(X_1 = x_1, X_2 = x_2, \dots, X_n = x_n) \text{ (vector)}\]
The PMF of a discrete real-valued random variable \(X\) is a function that assigns a probability (or absolute likelihood) to each of the possible discrete values that \(X\) can take.

Key Properties:
\[\text{Non-Negativity: } p_X(x) \geq 0 \text{ for all } x \] \[\text{Sum: } \sum_x p_X(x) = 1 \] \[\text{Bounds: } 0 \leq p_X(x) \leq 1 \text{ for all } x \] \[\text{Relation to CDF: } F_X(x_n) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} p_X(x_i) \]
A PMF can be converted to a PDF by representing each \(p_X(x_i)\) as an impulse \(p_X(x_i)\delta(x - x_i)), which gives:
\[ f_X(x) = \sum_i p_X(x_i) \delta(x - x_i) \] \[ f_{XY}(x,y) = \sum_i \sum_j p_{XY}(x_i, y_j) \delta(x - x_i) \delta(y - y_j) \] \[ f_\mathbf{X}(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{x_{1i}} \sum_{x_{2i}} \dots \sum_{x_{ni}} p_\mathbf{X}(x_{1i}, x_{2i}, \dots, x_{ni}) \delta(x_1 - x_{1i}) \delta(x_2 - x_{2i}) \dots \delta(x_n - x_{ni}) \]
Probability Density Function (PDF)
\[ f_X(x) = \dfrac{d}{dx}F_X(x) \text{ (single)}\] \[ f_{XY}(x, y) = \dfrac{\partial ^ 2}{\partial x \partial y}F_{XY}(x, y) \text{ (joint)}\] \[ f_\mathbf{X}(\mathbf{x}) = \dfrac{\partial ^ n}{\partial x_1 \partial x_2 \dots \partial x_n} F_\mathbf{X}(\mathbf{x}) \text{ (vector)}\]
The PDF of a continuous real-valued random variable \(X\) describes the relative likelihood (rather than the absolute likelihood) of the various (infinite) values that \(X\) can take. It is used to specify the probability that the value of \(X\) falls within a certain range of possible values. If \(F_X(x)\) is the CDF of \(X\), then the PDF of \(X\) is the derivate of this CDF.

Key Properties:
\[\text{Non-Negativity: } f_X(x) \geq 0 \] \[\text{Integral: } \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} f_X(x) dx = 1 \] \[\text{Probability: } P(a \leq X \leq b) = \int_a^b f_X(x) dx \] \[\text{Infinitesimal Interpretation: } f_X(x)dx = P(x \leq X \leq x + dx) \] \[\text{Relation to CDF: } F_X(x_1)dx = \int_{-\infty}^{x_1} f_X(x) dx \]
Marginal CDF
\[ F_X(x) = F_{XY}(x,\infty) \] \[ F_{X_1}(x_1) = F_{\mathbf{X}}(x_1,\infty,\infty,\dots,\infty) \]
Marginal PMF
\[ p_X(x) = \sum_i p_{XY}(x, y_i) \] \[ p_{X_1}(x_1) = \sum_{x_{2i}} \sum_{x_{3i}} \dots \sum_{x_{ni}} \ p_{\mathbf{X}}(x_1, x_{2i}, x_{3i}, \dots, x_{ni}) \]
Marginal PDF
\[ f_X(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{XY}(x,y) dy \] \[ f_{X_1}(x_1) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \dots \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{\mathbf{X}}(\mathbf{x}) dx_2 dx_3 \dots dx_n \]
Chain Rule for PDFs
\[ f_{\mathbf{X}}(\mathbf{x}) = f_{X_n}(x_n|x_1, x_2, \dots, x_{n-1}) f_{X_{n-1}}(x_{n-1}|x_1, x_2, \dots, x_{n-2}) \dots f_{X_2}(x_2 | x_1) f_{X_1}(x_1) \]
Distributions of Independent Random Variables
\[ f_{X}(x|y) = f_X(x) \Rightarrow f_{XY}(x,y) = f_X(x) f_Y(y) \] \[ p_{X}(x|y) = p_X(x) \Rightarrow p_{XY}(x,y) = p_X(x) p_Y(y) \]
Independence also implies the following: \[ F_{XY}(x,y) = F_X(x) F_Y(y) \] \[ F_{X}(x|Y=y) = F_X(x), \quad F_{Y}(y|X=x) = F_Y(y) \] In the vector variable case, the collection of random variables are independent if: \[ f_{\mathbf{X}}(\mathbf{x}) = f_{X_1}(x_1) f_{X_2}(x_2) \dots f_{X_n}(x_n) \] This implies: \[ f_{X_1}(x_1|x_2, x_3, \dots x_n) = f_{X_1}(x_1) \]
Central Limit Theorem
\[ \text{Let } \mathbf{X} = \{ X_1, X_2, \dots, X_n \} \text{ be a sequence of } \mathbf{IID \ random \ variables} \text{.} \] \[ \text{Let the IID variables have a shared mean } \mu \text{ and variance } \sigma^2 \text{.} \] \[ \text{Then } \bar{\mathbf{X}} = \dfrac{X_1 + X_2 + \dots + X_n}{n} \text{ is itself a random variable.} \] \[ \text{The } \mathbf{Central \ Limit \ Theorem} \text{ states:} \] \[ \text{As } n \to \infty \text{, } Z_n = \dfrac{\bar{\mathbf{X}} - \mu}{\sigma_\bar{\mathbf{X}}} \to Z \text{, where } Z \sim N(0,1) \text{ and } \sigma_\bar{\mathbf{X}} = \dfrac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \text{.} \]

Conditioning Distributions ▼

Joint CDF Conditioned on an Interval
\[ F_{XY}(x, y|Y \leq y_1) = \dfrac{F_{XY}(x,y)}{F_Y(y_1)} \]
When you condition a joint CDF on a variable being within an interval, the size of the CDF is reduced but the dimensionality remains the same. The visual intuition behind this can be understood in the case of two random variables. The 2D surface described by \( F_{XY}(x,y) \) is zero along the lines \( X = x_{min} \) and \( Y = y_{min} \). It starts at a height of zero at the point \( (x_{min}, y_{min}) \), and increases monotonically to a height of one at the point \( (x_{max}, y_{max}) \). When the CDF is conditioned on \( Y \leq y_1 \), this can be visually interpreted as follows:

1. Chop off the 2D CDF surface for \( Y > y_1\).
2. Find the peak value of the remaining monotonically increasing surface at \( (x_{max}, y_1) \), which is given by \(F_Y(y_1)\) since \( F_Y(y_1) = F_{XY}(x_{max}, y_1) \).
3. Normalise the surface so that the new peak value is at a height of one again by dividing each point on the surface by \(F_Y(y_1)\).

Thus, the size of the joint CDF is reduced but it is still two-dimensional.
Joint CDF Conditioned on a Value
\[ F_{X}(x|Y=y_1) = \dfrac{F_{XY}(x,y_1)}{F_Y(y_1)} \]
When you condition a joint CDF on a variable taking a particular value, the dimensionality of the CDF is reduced. The visual intuition behind this can be understood in the case of two random variables. The 2D surface described by \( F_{XY}(x,y) \) is zero along the lines \( X = x_{min} \) and \( Y = y_{min} \). It starts at a height of zero at the point \( (x_{min}, y_{min}) \), and increases monotonically to a height of one at the point \( (x_{max}, y_{max}) \). When the CDF is conditioned on \( Y \leq y_1 \), this can be visually interpreted as follows:

1. Vertically slice the 2D surface along the line \( Y = y_1 \), and consider the 1D curve that results from the cross-section.
2. Find the peak value of the remaining monotonically increasing curve at \( x_{max} \), which is given by \(F_Y(y_1)\) since \( F_Y(y_1) = F_{XY}(x_{max}, y_1) \).
3. Normalise the curve so that the new peak value is at a height of one again by dividing each point on the curve by \(F_Y(y_1)\).

Thus, the result is a marginal CDF that is only one-dimensional.
Joint PMF Conditioned on an Interval
\[ p_{XY}(x,y|Y \leq y_k) = \dfrac{p_{XY}(x,y)}{F_{Y}(y_k)} \]
When you condition a joint PMF on a variable being within an interval, the size of the PMF is reduced but the dimensionality remains the same. The visual intuition behind this can be understood in the case of two random variables. The 2D array of points described by \( p_{XY}(x,y) \) represents the probability of different value pairs the random variables can take, and the heights of all the points sum to one. When the PMF is conditioned on \( Y \leq y_k \), this can be visually interpreted as follows:

1. Chop off the 2D PMF array for rows where \( Y > y_k\).
2. Find the sum of the heights of the remaining points, which is given by \(F_Y(y_k)\) since \( F_Y(y_k) = F_{XY}(x_{max}, y_k) = \sum_{y_{min}}^{y_k} \sum_{x_{min}}^{x_{max}} p_{XY}(x, y) \).
3. Normalise the array so that the new sum of the point heights equals one again by dividing each point on the surface by \(F_Y(y_k)\).

Thus, the size of the joint PMF is reduced but it is still two-dimensional.
Joint PMF Conditioned on a Value
\[ p_X(x|Y=y_k) = \dfrac{p_{XY}(x,y_k)}{p_Y(y_k)} \]
When you condition a joint PMF on a variable taking a particular value, the dimensionality of the PMF is reduced. The visual intuition behind this can be understood in the case of two random variables. The 2D array of points described by \( p_{XY}(x,y) \) represents the probability of different value pairs the random variables can take, and the heights of all the points sum to one. When the PMF is conditioned on \( Y = y_k \), this can be visually interpreted as follows:

1. Extract the row of points along the line \( Y = y_k \).
2. Find the sum of the heights of these points with \( p_Y(y_k) \), since \( p_Y(y_k) = \sum_{x_{min}}^{x_{max}} p_{XY}(x,y_k) \).
3. Normalise the points so that their heights sum to one by dividing each point on the curve by \( p_Y(y_k) \).

Thus, the result is a marginal PMF that is only one-dimensional.
Joint PDF Conditioned on an Interval
\[ f_{XY}(x, y|Y \leq y_1) = \dfrac{f_{XY}(x,y)}{F_Y(y_1)} \]
When you condition a joint PDF on a variable being within an interval, the size of the PDF is reduced but the dimensionality remains the same. The visual intuition behind this can be understood in the case of two random variables. The 2D surface described by \( f_{XY}(x,y) \) represents the relative likelihood of different value pairs the random variables can take, and the total volume under the surface is equal to one. When the PDF is conditioned on \( Y \leq y_1 \), this can be visually interpreted as follows:

1. Chop off the 2D PDF surface for \( Y > y_1\).
2. Find the volume under the remaining surface, which is given by \(F_Y(y_1)\) since \( F_Y(y_1) = F_{XY}(x_{max}, y_1) = \int_{y_{min}}^{y_1} \int_{x_{min}}^{x_{max}} f_{XY}(x, y) dx dy \).
3. Normalise the surface so that the new volume under the surface equals one again by dividing each point on the surface by \(F_Y(y_1)\).

Thus, the size of the joint PDF is reduced but it is still two-dimensional.
Joint PDF Conditioned on a Value
\[ f_{X}(x|Y=y_1) = \dfrac{f_{XY}(x,y_1)}{f_Y(y_1)} \]
When you condition a joint PDF on a variable taking a particular value, the dimensionality of the PDF is reduced. The visual intuition behind this can be understood in the case of two random variables. The 2D surface described by \( f_{XY}(x,y) \) represents the relative likelihood of different value pairs the random variables can take, and the total volume under the surface is equal to one. When the PDF is conditioned on \( Y = y_1 \), this can be visually interpreted as follows:

1. Vertically slice the 2D surface along the line \( Y = y_1 \), and consider the 1D curve that results from the cross-section.
2. Find the area under this 1D curve by computing \( f_Y(y_1) \), since \( f_Y(y_1) = \int_{x_{min}}^{x_{max}} f_{XY}(x,y_1) dx \).
3. Normalise the curve so that the new area under the curve is equal to one by dividing each point on the curve by \( f_Y(y_1) \).

Thus, the result is a marginal PDF that is only one-dimensional.

Descriptive Statistics ▼

Mean
\[\begin{align} \mu_X &= E[X] \\ &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x f_X(x) dx \\ &= \sum_{i = 1}^{\infty} x_i p_X(x_i) \end{align}\]
The mean of a random variable can be thought of as an average of all the random variable's possible values weighted by their probabilities. It is equivalent to the first raw moment of the random variable's distribution.

The expected value operator \( E[\dot]\) is linear and the expectation of a constant is the constant itself. This means that \(E[aX + bY + c] = aE[X] + bE[Y] + c\).

The mean or expectation can also be extended to a mean vector when there are more than one random variable: \[ \boldsymbol{\mu}_\mathbf{X} = E[\mathbf{X}] = E\begin{bmatrix} X_1 \\ X_2 \\ \vdots \\ X_n \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} E[X_1] \\ E[X_2] \\ \vdots \\ E[X_n] \end{bmatrix} \]
Variance
\[\begin{align} \sigma_X^2 &= E[(X - \mu_X) ^ 2] \\ &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} (x - \mu_X) ^ 2 f_X(x) dx \\ &= \sum_{i = 1}^{\infty} (x_i - \mu_X) ^ 2 p_X(x_i) \end{align}\]
The variance of a random variable quantifies the spread of outcomes around the mean outcome. It is the expected value of the squared devation of outcomes from the mean. It is equivalent to the second central moment of the random variable's distribution.

Note that \(\mu_X = E[X]\) implies following identity: \[ \begin{align} \sigma_X^2 &= E[(X - E[X])^2] \\ &= E[X^2 - 2XE[X] + E[X]^2] \\ &= E[X^2] - 2E[X]E[X] + E[X]^2 \\ &= E[X^2] - 2E[X]^2 + E[X]^2 \\ &= E[X^2] - E[X]^2 \end{align}\] The variance of \(X\) can also be expressed as \(Var[X]\). To consider the effect of a change of scale and origin, let \(Y = aX + b\). The mean of \(Y\) is given by \( \mu_Y = a\mu_X + b\). The variance of \(Y\) is given by \(Var[Y] = E[(aX + b - a \mu_X - b)^2] = a^2 E[(X - mu_X)^2] = a^2 Var[X] \). Therefore, \[ Var[cX] = c^2 Var[X] \] \[ Var[X+c] = Var[X] \] \[ Var[c] = 0 \] The variance of a vector of random variables \(\mathbf{X}\) is given by the main diagonal of the covariance matrix \(\mathbf{C}_\mathbf{X}\).
\(n\)-th Raw Moment
\[\begin{align} m_n &= E[X ^ n] \\ &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x ^ n f_X(x) dx \\ &= \sum_{i = 1}^{\infty} x_i ^ m p_X(x_i) \end{align}\]
The \(n\)-th raw moment is a generalisation of the operation performed to compute the mean. They are a set of quantities that describe the shape of a probability distribution about the origin.
\(n\)-th Central Moment
\[\begin{align} c_n &= E[(X - E[X]) ^ n] \\ &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} (x - m_1) ^ n f_X(x) dx \\ &= \sum_{i = 1}^{\infty} (x_i - m_1) ^ n p_X(x_i) \end{align}\]
The \(n\)-th central moment is a generalisation of the operation performed to compute the variance. They are a set of quantities that describe the shape of a probability distribution about its average or mean value.
Correlation
\[ R_{XY} = E[XY] \]
Technically, this is the second-order raw moment of \(X\) and \(Y\), but it is often referred to as the correlation between \(X\) and \(Y\) in engineering applications such a signal processing.
Correlation Matrix
\[ \begin{align} \mathbf{R}_\mathbf{X} &= E[\mathbf{X}\mathbf{X}^T] \\ &= \begin{bmatrix} R_{11} & R_{12} & \dots & R_{1n} \\ R_{21} & R_{22} & \dots & R_{2n} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ R_{n1} & R_{n2} & \dots & R_{nn} \end{bmatrix} \end{align}\]
Note that the correlation matrix \( \mathbf{R}_\mathbf{X} \) is of size \( n \times n \) and is symmetric, so \( R_{ij} = R_{ji} \).
Covariance
\[ \begin{align} C_{XY} &= E[(X - E[X])(Y - E[Y])] \\ &= E[XY] - E[X]E[Y] \\ &= R_{XY} - \mu_X \mu_Y \end{align}\]
The alternative expressions for covariance can be derived as follows: \[ \begin{align} C_{XY} &= E[(X - E[X])(Y - E[Y])] \\ &= E[XY - XE[Y] - YE[X] + E[X]E[Y]] \\ &= E[XY] - E[X]E[Y] - E[Y]E[X] + E[X]E[Y] \\ &= E[XY] - E[X]E[Y] \\ &= R_{XY} - \mu_X \mu_Y \end{align}\]
Covariance Matrix
\[ \begin{align} \mathbf{C}_\mathbf{X} &= E[(\mathbf{X} - \boldsymbol{\mu}_\mathbf{X})(\mathbf{X} - \boldsymbol{\mu}_\mathbf{X})^T] \\ &= \mathbf{R}_\mathbf{X} - \boldsymbol{\mu}_\mathbf{X} \boldsymbol{\mu}_\mathbf{X}^T \\ &= \begin{bmatrix} C_{11} & C_{12} & \dots & C_{1n} \\ C_{21} & C_{22} & \dots & C_{2n} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ C_{n1} & C_{n2} & \dots & C_{nn} \end{bmatrix} \end{align}\]
Note that the covariance matrix \( \mathbf{C}_\mathbf{X} \) is of size \( n \times n \) and is symmetric, so \( C_{ij} = C_{ji} \). Also note that \( \{ C_{ii} \}_{i=1}^{n} \) are the variances of the random variables in \(\mathbf{X}\), and \( \{ C_{ij} \}_{i \neq j} \) are the covariances between each pair of random variables in \(\mathbf{X}\).
Pearson Correlation Coefficient
\[ \rho_{XY} = \dfrac{C_{XY}}{\sigma_X \sigma_Y} \]
Note that \( |\rho_{XY}| \leq 1 \) since Pearson's correlation coefficient is a normalised measure of correlation. It indicates the strength and direction of the linear relationship between \(X\) and \(Y\).

If \( \rho_{XY} = 1 \) exactly, then there is a perfect positive linear relationship between the values of \(X\) and \(Y\). If \( \rho_{XY} = -1 \) exactly, then there is a perfect negative linear relationship between the values of \(X\) and \(Y\). In both cases, this means that knowing \(X\) allows \(Y\) to be perfectly estimated, provided the magnitude of the proportionality constant is known.

If \( \rho_{XY} = 0 \) then \(X\) and\(Y\) are uncorrelated. This means that knowing \(X\) does not allow \(Y\) to be linearly estimated at all.

If \( \rho_{XY} \) is any other value, it indicates that knowing \(X\) provides some information about \(Y\), but \(Y\) cannot be perfectly linearly estimated. There is a general trend between \(X\) and \(Y\), but the estimates of \(Y\) will have uncertainty. The linear relationship is stronger if \( \rho_{XY} \) is closer to one or negative one, and weaker if \( \rho_{XY} \) is closer to zero.
Orthogonality
\[ \text{If } R_{XY} = E[XY] = 0 \text{ then } X \text{ and } Y \text{ are } \mathbf{orthogonal} \text{.} \]
Conceptually, two random variables \(X\) and \(Y\) are orthogonal if the expectation of their inner product is zero. This means that, on average, the product of \(X\) and \(Y\) is zero.

Generally, orthogonality does not imply uncorrelatedness, and uncorrelatedness does not imply orthogonality. However, if \(X\) and \(Y\) are both zero-mean random variables, then \( E[X] = E[Y] = 0 \). This simplifies the correlation to \(C_{XY} = R_{XY} = E[XY]\). Therefore, orthogonality \(\Leftrightarrow\) uncorrelatedness if the random variables are both zero-mean, but being zero-mean does not automatically imply orthogonality or uncorrelatedness. In other words, orthogonality and uncorrelatedness are equivalent concepts in the case of zero-mean random variables.

If \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent, then \( E[XY] = E[X]E[Y] \). Therefore, independence does not automatically imply orthogonality unless both \(X\) and \(Y\) are zero-mean.
Uncorrelatedness
\[ \text{If } C_{XY} = E[XY] - E[X]E[Y] = 0 \text{ then } X \text{ and } Y \text{ are } \mathbf{uncorrelated} \text{.} \]
Conceptually, two random variables \(X\) and \(Y\) are uncorrelated if their covariance is zero. This means that there is no linear relationship between \(X\) and \(Y\), so knowing the value of \(X\) does not allow \(Y\) to be linearly predicted. In other words, knowing \(X\) gives you no linear information about \(Y\). It is important to note that uncorrelated random variables can still have non-linear relationships, like a quadratic relationship.

Generally, orthogonality does not imply uncorrelatedness, and uncorrelatedness does not imply orthogonality. However, if \(X\) and \(Y\) are both zero-mean random variables, then \( E[X] = E[Y] = 0 \). This simplifies the correlation to \(C_{XY} = R_{XY} = E[XY]\). Therefore, orthogonality \(\Leftrightarrow\) uncorrelatedness if the random variables are both zero-mean, but being zero-mean does not automatically imply orthogonality or uncorrelatedness. In other words, orthogonality and uncorrelatedness are equivalent concepts in the case of zero-mean random variables.

If \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent, then \( E[XY] = E[X]E[Y] \). Therefore, independence automatically implies uncorrelatedness, regardless of whether \(X\) and \(Y\) are zero-mean random variables. However, the converse is not true: two random variables \(X\) and \(Y\) being uncorrelated does not necessarily imply that \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent.
Independence
\[ \text{If } f_{XY}(x,y) = f_X(x)f_Y(y) \text{ then } X \text{ and } Y \text{ are } \mathbf{independent} \text{.} \]
Conceptually, two random variables \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent if knowing the value of one gives absolutely no information about the value of the other. If knowing \(X\) does not provide any information about \(Y\), then \(Y\) cannot be linearly estimated from \(X\), so \(X\) and \(Y\) must also be uncorrelated.

If \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent, then \( E[XY] = E[X]E[Y] \). However, \( E[XY] = E[X]E[Y] \) does not necessarily imply that \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent. Mathematically: \[ f_{XY}(x,y) = f_X(x)f_Y(y) \Rightarrow E[XY] = E[X]E[Y] \] \[ E[XY] = E[X]E[Y] \nRightarrow f_{XY}(x,y) = f_X(x)f_Y(y) \] Since \(E[XY] = E[X]E[Y]\) is the condition for uncorrelatedness, \(X\) and \(Y\) being independent automatically implies uncorrelatedness. However, the converse is not true: two random variables \(X\) and \(Y\) being uncorrelated does not necessarily imply that \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent.

1. The entire multivariate distribution is fully specified by the mean vector \(\boldsymbol{\mu}_\mathbf{x}\) and the correlation matrix \(\mathbf{C}_\mathbf{X}\).
2. Any marginal PDF computed from the joint PDF will also be normally distributed.
3. Any conditional PDF will also be normally distributed.
4. If the jointly normal random variables are uncorrelated, then they are also independent.
5. A linear transformation of a multivariate normal distribution is also a multivariate normal distribution.
6. The sum of multivariate normal distributions is also a multivariate normal distribution.

This simplification is made by firstly reflecting the secondary side impedance to the primary side, then shifting the shunt admittance to the input terminal, and then finally merging the primary impedance with the reflected secondary impedance. The shifting of the shunt branch introduces a slight inaccuracy. Open Circuit Test
\[ \boldsymbol{Y}_e = G_c - j B_m = \dfrac{\boldsymbol{I}_{OC}}{\boldsymbol{V}_{OC}} \] \[ |\boldsymbol{Y}_e| = \dfrac{I_{OC}}{V_{OC}} \] \[ \angle \boldsymbol{Y}_e = -\cos^{-1}{\left( \dfrac{P_{OC}}{V_{OC} I_{OC}} \right)} \]
Typically, the Open Circuit Test is performed by connecting the primary side to its rated voltage supply, leaving the secondary side open-circuited. The primary side voltage is set by the tester, and the input current and power are measured with an ammeter and wattmeter. Currents are kept low due to the open circuit condition and the voltage doesn't exceed the transformer's rated voltage. This ensures that the transformer isn't damaged whilst maximising the magnitude of the measurements (which reduces percentage error and uncertainty).

Note the minus sign in front of the admittance angle. This is because admittance is the reciprocal of impedance, and taking the reciprocal of a complex number flips the sign of its angle.

\[ P_{max} = \dfrac{V_R V_S}{X_{line}} \text{ when } \delta=90^\circ \]
This approximation assumes a lossless line (zero resistance). First, take the receiving voltage as a reference, so \(\angle \boldsymbol{V}_R = 0 ^\circ\). Then, the current in the line given by the following: \[ I_S = I_R = \dfrac{|V_S \angle \delta - V_R \angle 0^\circ |}{X_{line}} = \dfrac{\sqrt{(V_S \cos{\delta} - V_R)^2 + (V_S \sin{\delta})^2} }{X_{line}} \] At the receiving end of the transmission line, the output power is given by the following: \[ P_R = V_R I_R \cos{\theta} \] Combining these equations gives: \[ P_R = V_R \dfrac{\sqrt{(V_S \cos{\delta} - V_R)^2 + (V_S \sin{\delta})^2} }{X_{line}} \cos{\theta} \] It can be seen that \(P_R\) is maximised if \(\delta = 90 ^\circ\). This should make sense mathematically, since the current is dependent on the difference of two voltage phasors. If the voltage phasors are orthogonal, then there is no cancellation of components, so the magnitude of the phasor difference is maximised.

For long transmission lines, the distributed nature of the line's electrical properties must be properly considered if an accurate model is to be developed. The transmission line can be considered a cascade of infinitesimal transmission line sections. The infinitesimal sections can be modelled using phasors with the following equivalent circuit:


The quantities \(R\), \(L\), \(G\), and \(C\) are specified per unit length, and they are constant along the length of the line. The transmission line's impedance and admittance per unit length can be computed from these quantities as follows: \[ z(\omega) = R + j \omega L\] \[ y(\omega) = G + j \omega C\] The following two partial differential equations can be written for this equivalent circuit: \[ \dfrac{\partial}{\partial x} \boldsymbol{V}(x, \omega) = -z(\omega) \boldsymbol{I}(x, \omega) \] \[ \dfrac{\partial}{\partial x} \boldsymbol{I}(x, \omega) = -y(\omega) \boldsymbol{V}(x, \omega) \] Intuitively, the rate at which the voltage drops along the line at a certain point \(x\) is proportional to both the per-unit series impedance and the current through that impedance at that point. Similarly, the rate at which the series current drops along the line at a certain point \(x\) is proportional to both the per-unit shunt admittance and the voltage across that admittanec at that point. For a given constant frequency, the equations simplify to the following ordinary differential equations: \[ \dfrac{d}{d x} \boldsymbol{V}(x) = -z \boldsymbol{I}(x) \] \[ \dfrac{d}{d x} \boldsymbol{I}(x) = -y \boldsymbol{V}(x) \] By differentiating one equation and substituting in the other, either the voltage phasors or current phasors can be eliminated, reducing the system to only one dependent variable. \[ \dfrac{d^2}{d x^2} \boldsymbol{V}(x) = zy \boldsymbol{V}(x) \] \[ \dfrac{d^2}{d x^2} \boldsymbol{I}(x) = yz \boldsymbol{I}(x) \] By defining the propagation constant as \(\boldsymbol{\gamma} = \sqrt{zy}\) and rearranging, these equations can be rewritten as the following homogeneous ODEs: \[ \dfrac{d^2}{d x^2} \boldsymbol{V}(x) - \boldsymbol{\gamma}^2 \boldsymbol{V}(x) = 0 \] \[ \dfrac{d^2}{d x^2} \boldsymbol{I}(x) - \boldsymbol{\gamma}^2 \boldsymbol{I}(x) = 0 \] By inspection, the voltage ODE has the following solution: \[ \boldsymbol{V}(x) = A_1 e^{\boldsymbol{\gamma} x} + A_2 e^{-\boldsymbol{\gamma} x} \] Differentiating this solution and substituiting it into the earlier first-order ODE gives the following: \[ \dfrac{d}{d x} \boldsymbol{V}(x) = \boldsymbol{\gamma} A_1 e^{\boldsymbol{\gamma} x} - \boldsymbol{\gamma} A_2 e^{-\boldsymbol{\gamma} x} = -z \boldsymbol{I}(x) \] \[ \boldsymbol{I}(x) = \dfrac{\boldsymbol{\gamma} A_1 e^{\boldsymbol{\gamma} x} - \boldsymbol{\gamma} A_2 e^{-\boldsymbol{\gamma} x}}{-z} = \dfrac{A_2 e^{\boldsymbol{-\gamma} x} - A_1 e^{\boldsymbol{\gamma} x}}{z / \boldsymbol{\gamma}} \] By defining the characteristic impedance as \(\boldsymbol{Z}_c = z / \boldsymbol{\gamma} = \sqrt{z / y} \), this is rewritten as: \[ \boldsymbol{I}(x) = \dfrac{A_2 e^{-\boldsymbol{\gamma} x} - A_1 e^{\boldsymbol{\gamma} x}}{\boldsymbol{Z}_c} \] At \(x=0\), the voltage and current phasors are defined as \(\boldsymbol{V}_S\) and \(\boldsymbol{I}_S\) respectively. Substituting these boundary conditions gives: \[ \boldsymbol{V}_S = A_1 + A_2 \] \[ \boldsymbol{I}_S = \dfrac{A_2 - A_1}{\boldsymbol{Z}_c} \] Solving for the coefficients gives: \[ A_1 = \dfrac{\boldsymbol{V}_S - \boldsymbol{I}_S \boldsymbol{Z}_c}{2} \] \[ A_2 = \dfrac{\boldsymbol{V}_S + \boldsymbol{I}_S \boldsymbol{Z}_c}{2} \] Substituting these results into the equations gives: \[ \boldsymbol{V}(x) = \dfrac{\boldsymbol{V}_S - \boldsymbol{I}_S \boldsymbol{Z}_c}{2} e^{\boldsymbol{\gamma} x} + \dfrac{\boldsymbol{V}_S + \boldsymbol{I}_S \boldsymbol{Z}_c}{2} e^{-\boldsymbol{\gamma} x} \] \[ \boldsymbol{I}(x) = \dfrac{\boldsymbol{V}_S + \boldsymbol{I}_S \boldsymbol{Z}_c}{2\boldsymbol{Z}_c} e^{-\boldsymbol{\gamma} x} - \dfrac{\boldsymbol{V}_S - \boldsymbol{I}_S \boldsymbol{Z}_c}{2\boldsymbol{Z}_c} e^{\boldsymbol{\gamma} x} \] If the transmission line has length \(l\), then evaluating these equations at \(x=l\) will give \(\boldsymbol{V}_R\) and \(\boldsymbol{I}_R\). Evaluating and regrouping gives: \[ \boldsymbol{V}_R = \left(\dfrac{e^{\boldsymbol{\gamma} l} + e^{-\boldsymbol{\gamma} l}}{2}\right) \boldsymbol{V}_S + \left( \boldsymbol{Z}_c \dfrac{e^{-\boldsymbol{\gamma} l} - e^{\boldsymbol{\gamma} l}}{2} \right) \boldsymbol{I}_S \] \[ \boldsymbol{I}_R = \left( \dfrac{1}{\boldsymbol{Z}_c} \dfrac{e^{-\boldsymbol{\gamma} l} - e^{\boldsymbol{\gamma} l}}{2} \right) \boldsymbol{V}_S + \left( \dfrac{e^{\boldsymbol{\gamma} l} + e^{-\boldsymbol{\gamma} l}}{2} \right) \boldsymbol{I}_S \] The hyperbolic sine and cosine are defined as follows: \[ \sinh{(x)} = \dfrac{e^x - e^{-x}}{2} \] \[ \cosh{(x)} = \dfrac{e^x + e^{-x}}{2} \] Therefore, the equations above can be rewritten as follows: \[ \boldsymbol{V}_R = \cosh{(\boldsymbol{\gamma}l)} \boldsymbol{V}_S - \boldsymbol{Z}_c \sinh{(\boldsymbol{\gamma}l)} \boldsymbol{I}_S \] \[ \boldsymbol{I}_R = -\dfrac{1}{\boldsymbol{Z}_c} \sinh{(\boldsymbol{\gamma}l)} \boldsymbol{V}_S + \cosh{(\boldsymbol{\gamma}l)} \boldsymbol{I}_S \] In matrix form: \[\begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{V}_R \\ \boldsymbol{I}_R \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \cosh{(\boldsymbol{\gamma}l)} & - \boldsymbol{Z}_c \sinh{(\boldsymbol{\gamma}l)} \\ - \dfrac{1}{\boldsymbol{Z}_c} \sinh{(\boldsymbol{\gamma}l)} & \cosh{(\boldsymbol{\gamma}l)} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{V}_S \\ \boldsymbol{I}_S \end{bmatrix} \] For ABCD models of two-port networks, the input or sending port variables are usually expressed in terms of the output or receiving port variables. To do this, the transmission matrix in the equation above must be inverted. However, this network is symmetric (since \(A = D\)) and reciprocal (since it only contains RLC elements, which means \(AD - BC = 1\)). Therefore, the ABCD model is simply given by: \[\begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{V}_S \\ \boldsymbol{I}_S \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \cosh{(\boldsymbol{\gamma}l)} & \boldsymbol{Z}_c \sinh{(\boldsymbol{\gamma}l)} \\ \dfrac{1}{\boldsymbol{Z}_c} \sinh{(\boldsymbol{\gamma}l)} & \cosh{(\boldsymbol{\gamma}l)} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{V}_R \\ \boldsymbol{I}_R \end{bmatrix} \]

The following equations can be used to compute the hyperbolic trig functions with complex arguments on a scientific calculator: \[ \cosh{(x+jy)} = \cosh{(x)}\cos{(y)} + j \sinh{(x)}\sin{(y)} \] \[ \sinh{(x+jy)} = \sinh{(x)}\cos{(y)} + j \cosh{(x)}\sin{(y)} \] \[ \tanh{(x+jy)} = \dfrac{\sinh{(x+jy)}}{\cosh{(x+jy)}} \]

System Matrix Equation
\[ \boldsymbol{I} = \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{I}_1 \\ \boldsymbol{I}_2 \\ \vdots \\ \boldsymbol{I}_n \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{Y}_{11} & \boldsymbol{Y}_{12} & \dots & \boldsymbol{Y}_{1n} \\ \boldsymbol{Y}_{21} & \boldsymbol{Y}_{22} & \dots & \boldsymbol{Y}_{2n} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \boldsymbol{Y}_{n1} & \boldsymbol{Y}_{n2} & \dots & \boldsymbol{Y}_{nn} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{V}_1 \\ \boldsymbol{V}_2 \\ \vdots \\ \boldsymbol{V}_n \end{bmatrix} = \boldsymbol{Y} \boldsymbol{V} \]
Admittance Matrix Elements
\[ \boldsymbol{Y}_{ik} = \begin{cases} -y_{ik} & \text{if } i \neq k, \\ \displaystyle{\sum_{k=0, \ k \neq i}^{n}} y_{ik} & \text{if } i = k. \end{cases} \]
Here, \(y_{ik}\) is the admittance between bus \(i\) and bus \(k\). Hence, the diagonal elements of the admittance matrix \(\boldsymbol{Y}\) represent each bus's self-admittance, and the off-diagonal elements represent the mutual admittance between each possible pairing of the buses.

Note that if bus \(i\) has a generator with a series impedance, then \(y_{i0}\) is that series impedance converted to a shunt admittance. These generator admittances are included in the self-admittances \(Y_{ii}\) of the buses with generators. If there is no generator, then \(y_{i0} = 0\) and the indexing effectively starts from \(k=1\). This allows the series impedance of the generator to be easily incorporated into the power flow analysis.
Bus Current-Voltage Equation
\[ \boldsymbol{I}_i = \sum_{k=1}^n \boldsymbol{I}_{ik} = \sum_{k = 1}^{n} \boldsymbol{Y}_{ik} \boldsymbol{V}_{k} \]
Power Balance Equations
(Gauss-Seidel Format)
\[ \boldsymbol{V}_i = \dfrac{1}{\boldsymbol{Y}_{ii}} \left( \dfrac{P_i - j Q_i}{\boldsymbol{V}_i^*} - \sum_{k=1}^{n} \boldsymbol{Y}_{ik} \boldsymbol{V}_k \right) \] \[ Q_i = -\mathrm{Im} \left( \boldsymbol{V}_i^* \sum_{k=1}^{n} \boldsymbol{Y}_{ik} \boldsymbol{V}_k \right) \]
The net complex power generated at bus \(i\) is given by: \[ \boldsymbol{S}_i = \boldsymbol{V}_i \sum_{k=1}^{n} \boldsymbol{I}_{ik}^* \] Conjugating gives: \[ \begin{align} \boldsymbol{S}_i^* &= \boldsymbol{V}_i^* \sum_{k=1}^{n} \boldsymbol{I}_{ik} \\ &= \boldsymbol{V}_i^* \sum_{k=1}^{n} \boldsymbol{Y}_{ik} \boldsymbol{V}_k \\ &= \boldsymbol{V}_i^* \left( \boldsymbol{Y}_{ii} \boldsymbol{V}_i + \sum_{k=1, \ k \neq i}^{n} \boldsymbol{Y}_{ik} \boldsymbol{V}_k \right) \end{align} \] Dividing by \(\boldsymbol{V}_i^*\) gives: \[ \dfrac{\boldsymbol{S}_i^*}{\boldsymbol{V}_i^*} = \boldsymbol{Y}_{ii} \boldsymbol{V}_i + \sum_{k=1, \ k \neq i}^{n} \boldsymbol{Y}_{ik} \boldsymbol{V}_k \] Subtracting the summation term and then dividing by \(\boldsymbol{Y}_{ii}\) gives: \[ \boldsymbol{V}_i = \dfrac{1}{\boldsymbol{Y}_{ii}} \left( \dfrac{\boldsymbol{S}^*}{\boldsymbol{V}_i^*} - \sum_{k=1}^{n} \boldsymbol{Y}_{ik} \boldsymbol{V}_k \right) \]

Further, the conjugated complex power can be expressed as: \[ \boldsymbol{S}_i^* = P_i - j Q_i = \boldsymbol{V}_i^* \sum_{k=1}^{n} \boldsymbol{I}_{ik} \] Thus, it is clear that the reactive part \(Q_i\) is given by: \[ Q_i = -\mathrm{Im}(\boldsymbol{S}_i^*) = -\mathrm{Im} \left( \boldsymbol{V}_i^* \sum_{k=1}^{n} \boldsymbol{Y}_{ik} \boldsymbol{V}_k \right) \]

(Newton-Raphson Format)
\[ \begin{align} 0 &= P_i - (P_{Gi} - P_{Di}) \\ &= \sum_{k=1}^{n} |\boldsymbol{V}_i| |\boldsymbol{V}_k| ( G_{ik} \cos{\theta_{ik}} + B_{ik} \sin{\theta_{ik}} ) - (P_{Gi} - P_{Di}) \end{align} \] \[ \begin{align} 0 &= Q_i - (Q_{Gi} - Q_{Di}) \\ &= \sum_{k=1}^{n} |\boldsymbol{V}_i| |\boldsymbol{V}_k| ( G_{ik} \sin{\theta_{ik}} - B_{ik} \cos{\theta_{ik}} ) - (P_{Gi} - P_{Di}) \end{align} \]
\(P_i\) and \(Q_i\) are the net active and reactive power being generated at bus \(i\). \(P_{Gi}\) and \(P_{Di}\) are positive quantities that represent the active power generation and demand at bus \(i\) respectively. Similarly, \(Q_{Gi}\) and \(Q_{Di}\) are positive quantities that represent the reactive power generation and demand at bus \(i\) respectively. For there to be equilibrium, any net generation at bus \(i\) must be transmitted from bus \(i\) via the transmission lines connected to bus \(i\). Conversely, any net demand at the bus must be supplied through the bus's transmission lines. These equations are derived as follows: \[ \begin{align} \boldsymbol{S}_i &= \boldsymbol{V}_i \sum_{k=1}^{n} \boldsymbol{I}_{ik}^* \\ &= \boldsymbol{V}_i \sum_{k=1}^{n} \boldsymbol{Y}_{ik}^* \boldsymbol{V}_{k}^* \\ &= \boldsymbol{V}_i \sum_{k=1}^{n} (G_{ik} - j B_{ik}) \boldsymbol{V}_{k}^* \\ &= |\boldsymbol{V}_i| e^{j\theta_i} \sum_{k=1}^{n} (G_{ik} - j B_{ik}) |\boldsymbol{V}_{k}| e^{-j\theta_k} \\ &= \sum_{k=1}^{n} |\boldsymbol{V}_i| |\boldsymbol{V}_{k}| (G_{ik} - j B_{ik}) e^{j(\theta_i - \theta_k)} \\ &= \sum_{k=1}^{n} |\boldsymbol{V}_i| |\boldsymbol{V}_{k}| (G_{ik} - j B_{ik}) e^{j\theta_{ik}} \\ &= \sum_{k=1}^{n} |\boldsymbol{V}_i| |\boldsymbol{V}_{k}| (G_{ik} - j B_{ik}) ( \cos{\theta_{ik}} + j \sin{\theta_{ik}}) \\ &= \sum_{k=1}^{n} |\boldsymbol{V}_i| |\boldsymbol{V}_{k}| (G_{ik} \cos{\theta_{ik}} + j G_{ik} \sin{\theta_{ik}} - j B_{ik} \cos{\theta_{ik}} + B_{ik} \sin{\theta_{ik}}) \\ &= \sum_{k=1}^{n} |\boldsymbol{V}_i| |\boldsymbol{V}_k| ( G_{ik} \cos{\theta_{ik}} + B_{ik} \sin{\theta_{ik}} ) + j \sum_{k=1}^{n} |\boldsymbol{V}_i| |\boldsymbol{V}_k| ( G_{ik} \sin{\theta_{ik}} - B_{ik} \cos{\theta_{ik}} ) \\ &= P_i + j Q_i \end{align} \] The Newton-Raphson format of the power balance equations can then be formed by equating these expressions for \(P_i\) and \(Q_i\) to the difference between generation and demand at bus \(i\).

\[ \text{For any arbitrary three phasors } \{\boldsymbol{V}_a, \boldsymbol{V}_b, \boldsymbol{V}_c\} \] \[ \begin{align} \boldsymbol{V}_a &= \boldsymbol{V}_a^0 + \boldsymbol{V}_a^+ + \boldsymbol{V}_a^- \\ \boldsymbol{V}_b &= \boldsymbol{V}_b^0 + \boldsymbol{V}_b^+ + \boldsymbol{V}_b^- \\ \boldsymbol{V}_c &= \boldsymbol{V}_c^0 + \boldsymbol{V}_c^+ + \boldsymbol{V}_c^- \end{align} \] \[ \text{Where, if } \alpha = 1 \angle 120^\circ \text{, then} \] \[ \begin{align} & \boldsymbol{V}_a^0 = \boldsymbol{V}_b^0 = \boldsymbol{V}_c^0 \\ & \boldsymbol{V}_a^+ = \alpha \boldsymbol{V}_b^+ = \alpha^2 \boldsymbol{V}_c^+ \\ & \boldsymbol{V}_a^- = \alpha \boldsymbol{V}_c^- = \alpha^2 \boldsymbol{V}_b^- \end{align} \]
Sequence-to-Phase Transformation
\[ \mathbf{V}_P = \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{V}_a \\ \boldsymbol{V}_b \\ \boldsymbol{V}_c \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & \alpha^2 & \alpha \\ 1 & \alpha & \alpha^2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{V}_a^0 \\ \boldsymbol{V}_a^+ \\ \boldsymbol{V}_a^- \end{bmatrix} = \mathbf{A} \mathbf{V}_S \]
Phase-to-Sequence Transformation
\[ \mathbf{V}_S = \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{V}_a^0 \\ \boldsymbol{V}_a^+ \\ \boldsymbol{V}_a^- \end{bmatrix} = \dfrac{1}{3} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & \alpha & \alpha^2 \\ 1 & \alpha^2 & \alpha \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{V}_a \\ \boldsymbol{V}_b \\ \boldsymbol{V}_c \end{bmatrix} = \mathbf{A}^{-1} \mathbf{V}_P \]
Balanced Load Impedance Sequence Network
\[ \mathbf{V}_S = \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{V}_a^0 \\ \boldsymbol{V}_a^+ \\ \boldsymbol{V}_a^- \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{Z}_Y + 3\boldsymbol{Z}_N & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \boldsymbol{Z}_Y & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \boldsymbol{Z}_Y \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{I}_a^0 \\ \boldsymbol{I}_a^+ \\ \boldsymbol{I}_a^- \end{bmatrix} = \mathbf{Z}_S \mathbf{I}_S \]
If the loads are Y-connected and the neutral point is solidly grounded, then \( \boldsymbol{Z}_N = 0 \).

If the loads are \( \Delta \)-connected, then there is no neutral point. The loads can be converted to a Y-connected configuration with \( \boldsymbol{Z}_Y = \boldsymbol{Z}_\Delta / 3 \). In this case, the neutral point of the Y-connected equivalent load should not be connected to ground. This is represented by an infinite impedance, so \( \boldsymbol{Z}_N = \infty \).
Balanced Series Impedance Sequence Network
\[ \mathbf{V}_S = \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{V}_{aa'}^0 \\ \boldsymbol{V}_{aa'}^+ \\ \boldsymbol{V}_{aa'}^- \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{Z}_s + 2\boldsymbol{Z}_m & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \boldsymbol{Z}_s -\boldsymbol{Z}_m & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \boldsymbol{Z}_s - \boldsymbol{Z}_m \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{I}_a^0 \\ \boldsymbol{I}_a^+ \\ \boldsymbol{I}_a^- \end{bmatrix} = \mathbf{Z}_S \mathbf{I}_S \]
Synchronous Generator Sequence Network
\[ \mathbf{V}_S = \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{V}_{a}^0 \\ \boldsymbol{V}_{a}^+ \\ \boldsymbol{V}_{a}^- \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ \boldsymbol{E}_{g} \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{Z}_Y + 3\boldsymbol{Z}_N & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \boldsymbol{Z}_Y & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \boldsymbol{Z}_Y \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{I}_a^0 \\ \boldsymbol{I}_a^+ \\ \boldsymbol{I}_a^- \end{bmatrix} = \mathbf{Z}_S \mathbf{I}_S \]
Synchronous Motor Sequence Network
\[ \mathbf{V}_S = \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{V}_{a}^0 \\ \boldsymbol{V}_{a}^+ \\ \boldsymbol{V}_{a}^- \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ \boldsymbol{E}_{m} \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{Z}_Y + 3\boldsymbol{Z}_N & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \boldsymbol{Z}_Y & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \boldsymbol{Z}_Y \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{I}_a^0 \\ \boldsymbol{I}_a^+ \\ \boldsymbol{I}_a^- \end{bmatrix} = \mathbf{Z}_S \mathbf{I}_S \]

Asymmetrical Fault Analysis Process

Assumptions:

1. The power system is balanced and operating at steady-state before the fault, and a single fault causes the sequence networks to couple only at the fault location.
2. Since the fault currents are much larger than the normal load currents, the pre-fault load currents are neglected, which implies that all bus voltages and internal machine voltages are initially equal to \(V_F\).
3. The resistance of transformer windings and any transformer shunt admittances are neglected, so that only the series reactance of transformers are considered.
4. The resistance of transmission lines and any shunt admittances are neglected, so that only the series reactance of transmission lines are considered.
5. The armature resistance, saliency, and saturation of synchronous generators and motors are ignored.
6. All non-rotating loads are neglected by approximating the loads as open circuits.
7. Small induction motors are neglected, and large induction motors can be neglected or represented as synchronous motors are.

Economic Dispatch ▼

Economic Dispatch Problem
\[ \begin{align} & \text{Minimise:} \\ & C_T = \sum_{i=1}^{M} C_{Gi}(P_{Gi}) \end{align} \] \[ \begin{align} & \text{Such that:} \\ & P_G = P_D + P_{losses} \\ & P_{Gi,min} \leq P_{Gi} \leq P_{Gi,max} \end{align} \]
General Method of Lagrange Multipliers for a Single Constraint
\[ \text{To minimise } f(\mathbf{x}) \text{ subject to } g(\mathbf{x}) = 0 \text{, define: } \] \[ L(\mathbf{x}, \lambda) = f(\mathbf{x}) + \lambda g(\mathbf{x})\] \[ \text{Then minima and maxima occur when } \nabla L_\mathbf{x}(\mathbf{x}, \lambda) = 0 \text{ and } \nabla L_\lambda(\mathbf{x}, \lambda) = 0 \]
Economic Dispatch with Unconstrained Generation
\[ \lambda = \dfrac{dC_{G1}}{dP_{G1}} = \dfrac{dC_{G2}}{dP_{G2}} = \dots = \dfrac{dC_{Gm}}{dP_{Gm}} \] \[ P_{G1} + P_{G2} + \dots + P_{Gm} = P_D \]
Economic Dispatch with Constrained Generation
\[ \text{If } P_{Gi}(\lambda) > P_{Gi,max} \text{, then } P_{Gi} = P_{Gi,max} \] \[ \text{If } P_{Gi}(\lambda) < P_{Gi,min} \text{, then } P_{Gi} = P_{Gi,min} \]
Economic Dispatch with Losses
\[ L_i = 1 - \dfrac{\partial P_L(\mathbf{P}_G)}{\partial P_{Gi}} \] \[ \lambda = \dfrac{1}{L_1} \dfrac{dC_{G1}}{dP_{G1}} = \dfrac{1}{L_2} \dfrac{dC_{G2}}{dP_{G2}} = \dots = \dfrac{1}{L_m} \dfrac{dC_{Gm}}{dP_{Gm}} \]

Generator Electrical Model
\[ P_e(\delta) = \dfrac{E_a V_t}{X_d'} \sin{\delta} = P_{max} \sin{\delta} \]
\[ \boldsymbol{S}_e = \boldsymbol{V} \boldsymbol{I}^* = P_e + j Q_e\] \[ P_e = \mathrm{Re}(\boldsymbol{S}_e) \] \[ \begin{align} \boldsymbol{I} &= \dfrac{\boldsymbol{E}_a - \boldsymbol{V}_t}{jX_d'} \\ &= \dfrac{E_a \angle \delta - V_t \angle 0^\circ}{jX_d'} \\ &= \dfrac{E_a \angle \delta - V_t }{jX_d'} \\ &= \dfrac{E_a (\cos{\delta} + j \sin{\delta}) - V_t }{jX_d'} \\ &= \dfrac{E_a (\cos{\delta} / j + \sin{\delta}) - V_t / j }{X_d'} \\ &= \dfrac{ - j E_a \cos{\delta} + E_a \sin{\delta} + j V_t }{X_d'} \\ &= \dfrac{E_a \sin{\delta}}{X_d'} + j \dfrac{V_t - E_a \cos{\delta}}{X_d'} \end{align} \] \[ \boldsymbol{I}^* = \dfrac{E_a \sin{\delta}}{X_d'} - j \dfrac{V_t - E_a \cos{\delta}}{X_d'} \] \[ \begin{align} P_e &= \mathrm{Re}(\boldsymbol{S}_e) \\ &= \mathrm{Re}(\boldsymbol{V} \boldsymbol{I}^*) \\ &= \mathrm{Re}(V_t \angle 0^\circ \boldsymbol{I}^*) \\ &= \mathrm{Re}(V_t \boldsymbol{I}^*) \\ &= V_t \mathrm{Re}(\boldsymbol{I}^*) \\ &= \dfrac{E_a V_t}{X_d'} \sin{\delta} = P_{max} \sin{\delta} \end{align} \]
